The setup

They asked what to charge. The answer was what to include.

{{SETUP_LEAD}} — the pricing question arrived the way it always does: a number, a deadline, and three internal factions each certain about a different figure.

A price on its own is unanswerable. The same £34 is expensive for one specification and a bargain for another, and the only people who can settle which it is are the ones choosing between real alternatives with real money attached. So we stopped asking about price, and started making people choose.

What comes back is not a price. It is an exchange rate — how much of one thing people will give up to get more of another — and once you have that, the price mostly sets itself.

How the question was asked

Stated willingness-to-pay is a number people invent to sound reasonable. Asked directly, everyone is a careful shopper. Asked to choose, they behave like themselves. Twelve of these per respondent, every one a genuine trade: the fast delivery and the low price are never on the same card, and a choice still has to be made.

The “none” option never comes out. Delete it and every respondent is forced into a preference they may not hold, which quietly inflates every share estimate that follows. The design is balanced so each level appears equally often and no two attributes travel together — otherwise the model cannot tell which of the two a person was answering.

The exchange rate

What every feature is worth, in money.

What has to be paid for
Carrying the retailer’s own label−£3.10
Requiring an account at checkout−£5.80

Nobody was asked what they would pay for any of this. Every price here falls out of what people chose when they had to give something up, which is why the list rarely matches what the same people say in a survey. Read as an exchange rate: two days of delivery are worth more than three years of warranty — and asking for an account at checkout costs more than every improvement above the line is worth.
