The setup

Every brand in the category was competing on the same thing. All of them were wrong.

{{SETUP_LEAD}} — the category had spent a decade discounting, because a decade of surveys had said price was what mattered most. It said so every year, in every study, from every agency.

It said so because that is what the question makes people say. Asked directly whether price matters, nobody says no. Asked to rate ten attributes out of ten, people rate the ones they would be embarrassed to dismiss, and the result is a chart where everything is important and therefore nothing is.

What the client needed was not a ranking of what customers endorse. It was a ranking of what actually moves them — which can only be worked out from what they do, not from what they are willing to say.

Why we don’t ask

Ask people what matters, and they will tell you that everything does.

Price · Product quality · Delivery speed · Customer service · Range of choice · Website ease · Sustainability

That exhibit is real data, and it is worthless. Drawn on the scale respondents were actually given, seven attributes occupy eight tenths of one point, and half the scale is dead on arrival — nobody rates anything they were shown below a five. Rank that and the order is noise. Act on it and you are acting on rounding. We still field the question, because it catches anything catastrophically broken, but it is never allowed to decide.

Importance gets derived, not declared. We model actual satisfaction and actual choice against attribute-level experience, and read importance from how much each attribute moves the outcome when it varies. What people endorse is a claim. What predicts their behaviour is a lever.

And derived importance has to be defended. Attributes that travel together are tested for collinearity before either is credited; the model is fitted on part of the sample and checked on the part it has never seen. An attribute that only performs on the data it was built from does not make it into the chart.

Two attributes cross the whole chart. Delivery speed goes from fourth-most-endorsed to the single strongest predictor of whether someone comes back, and service climbs with it — both of them things customers describe as expected rather than important, which is exactly why they under-report them. Price falls to fourth. It still matters; it simply stops being the thing worth another point of margin.
