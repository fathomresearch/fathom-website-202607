The setup

Traffic was up. Awareness was up. Nothing else was.

{{SETUP_LEAD}} — the top of the funnel had been fed for two years, and the number at the bottom had barely moved. The instinct in the room was to feed it harder.

That instinct is expensive and it is usually wrong. A funnel that leaks doesn’t need more poured into it. Every extra visitor arrives at the same broken step, drops out at the same rate, and costs the same to acquire — so spending more buys a proportionally bigger loss.

The useful question isn’t how many people start. It’s where the ones who started stop, and whether that step is losing people for a reason anyone has actually looked at.

How the funnel was defined

Three systems, one step, three different numbers. That is where the work starts.

4,200
One agreed base
3,880

Deduplicated to people, not sessions, and counted at the same moment in every system. Written down, signed off, and used for every figure that follows.

Until the base is agreed, a drop-off rate is an opinion. Most funnel arguments we walk into are not disagreements about customers at all — they are two teams comparing numbers that were never counting the same thing. We reconcile first, in public, and we take the most conservative definition rather than the most flattering one.

Then we instrument the gaps. Systems record what they were built to bill for, which is rarely where people give up. Any step the stack can’t see gets covered another way — session replay, a short intercept, or a call to the people who abandoned it — before it is allowed into the chart.

Four of the five drops are ordinary — every funnel in the category loses people browsing, and losing a fifth of browsers before they choose is unremarkable. One drop is not ordinary. Two thirds of the people who had already chosen something walked away at the step immediately after choosing it, which is the one place in the sequence where intent is highest and the reason for leaving cannot be lack of interest.

The leak

{{LEAK_LINE}} — they weren’t deciding whether to buy. They’d decided. They were deciding whether to make an account.

The outcome

The same 3,880 people in. 1,130 more out the other end.
