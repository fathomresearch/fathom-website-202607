The flat score

Every stage scored between good and very good. The relationship was still leaking.

{{SETUP_SCORE}} — a healthy overall number, and underneath it, stage-level scores that never dropped below acceptable. Read as a table, there was nothing to fix.

That is what a table does to an experience. It rates each step against the step next to it, then averages the lot, and the average quietly assumes every moment carries the same weight. Customers don’t experience an average. They experience one thing after another, in an order, and they judge the whole relationship by how it felt at the two or three points where something was actually at stake.

So the question was never which stage scores worst. It was where the experience turns — which drop the customer is still carrying by the time they decide whether to come back.

The blind spots are the design. Transaction data knows precisely how long the wait was and nothing about what it felt like; a diary knows what it felt like and is unreliable about how long it really was. Every stage is covered twice, from two different kinds of evidence, so no stage is described by a single instrument’s idea of it.

And the stages come from customers, not the org chart. We build the sequence from people’s account of what happened next, then merge any steps they don’t experience as separate — however many teams own them. A journey map that mirrors your internal handoffs will always find the problem sitting neatly inside one team’s remit. Real ones rarely do.

06 · After
Living with it

Fine settles into forgettable — and forgettable is what makes the next choice open again.

Read as a table, stage four is unremarkable: it has no product to rate and no staff to score, so it collects the mildest numbers on the sheet. Read in order, it is the only place the line falls through neutral — and everything after it is recovery, not enjoyment. The stages either side were never the problem. They were carrying the damage done in between.
